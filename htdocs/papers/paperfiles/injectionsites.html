Abstract

With the opioid epidemic soaring in the United States, supervised injection sites are being applauded and opposed by many on both sides. These sites are enabling opioid addicts to use drugs in a highly controlled medical environment, to prevent overdose deaths, decrease the spread of disease, increase treatment outreach, and save lives. Some think, however, that these places are wasting medical resources by funding these sites, not just with equipment, but medical personnel also. Are we being helpful or hurtful with these sites? Are we helping save lives and improve treatments or are we just enabling addicts? This paper goes in-depth into the pros, cons, and politics of the supervised injection sites and if they help us or hurt us more when treating opioid addiction.

            Keywords: opioid epidemic, supervised injection sites, opioid addiction


 

 

Supervised injection sites across the world: Are we being helpful or hurtful?

A supervised injection site is a highly supervised, secure medical environment in which users of substances such as heroin and other drugs can use substances without criminal prosecution. These places offer access to sterile supplies, such as needles and tourniquets;  trained personnel with overdose medication to respond immediately to an overdose; and withdrawal management or detox services, such as methadone or suboxone treatment (Safe injection sites). The point of these sites is to help reduce the number of overdose deaths, decrease the spread of disease, and increase the threshold of individuals obtaining treatment for their substance use. However, some believe that it is a waste of medical resources and is an “enabling tool” for users of such substances to continue to use, just more easily than before. This is an analysis of both sides of the aisle.

What addiction is and how injection sites work

Life with addiction

130 people die every day from opioid overdose in the United States. You have a higher chance of dying from an opioid overdose than that of a car crash. Every year since 2010, the annual death rate has been over 400,000 deaths (Safe injection sites). With these deaths being so high, what can we do to help these addicts? Over 2 million people have opioid addiction in the United States (-------- & Hampton) and the different treatments can be scarce. Medication treatment for opioid addiction is hard to get according to Sarah Wakeman, M.D. from Harvard Medical School and Massachusetts General Hospital, as she says that only 5% of addicts received medication treatment in 2017 (------- & Hampton). What about counseling therapies? A former opioid addict and medical doctor at Harvard, Peter Grinspoon gives some insight. Grinspoon says he advocates for MAT or medication-assisted treatment, which brings behavioral therapy and medication together to treat addiction. However, while in rehab he realized many programs use an 'abstinence-only' model, which he says is a "holdover from the very beginnings of the recovery movement.” He also states that most treatment centers treat "every addiction the same" and are not using cutting-edge science. "You treat opiate withdrawal differently than you treat alcohol withdrawal" Grinspoon says (Schultz).

How injection sites help addicts

            Without safe injection sites, addicts usually will do drugs in a public area/place. In Toronto, 2018, after conservatives decided to close certain safe injection sites, 7 people had died within a 12 days and in 2016, there were 726 overdoses in Ontario (Lupick). When people do these drugs in public, it is dangerous not only for the public but for the users as well, because if they overdose, nobody will be there to revive them. The case for Vancouver, Vancouver Coastal Health says that at a safe injection site called Insite there were 8,317 overdose reversals, with zero deaths, and at a handful of prevention sites, 2,564 with zero deaths (Lupick). Combined altogether, more than 10,000 overdoses since Insite opened in 2003 have occurred without a single death (Lupick). Safe injection sites save lives, improve patient health, increase public safety, and increase engagement in treatment (Wakeman).

The core of addiction

            According to ASAM.org, addiction is, “a treatable, chronic medical disease involving complex interactions among brain circuits, genetics, the environment, and an individual's life experiences. People with addiction use substances or engage in behaviors that become compulsive and often continue despite harmful consequences” (Definition of addiction). Addiction is more than just a person choosing to do a drug, it’s a complex set of things that are hard to manage.

            Many drug users don’t want to be addicts, however, it’s a cycle that can be hard to break, especially depending on environmental factors, genetics, and more (Werb). The drive to want to end the cycle of addiction has to be achieved before an addict can ever end their addiction in the first place (Sharma).

Enabling drug users and legality

The argument for enabling drug users

Many people, especially in the United States, believe that supervised injection sites simply enable addicts to use again and again, while giving them an “incentive” to do so (Safe injection sites debate). This is a good argument, however, these sites aren’t giving individuals the drugs themselves, they are giving them clean needles and an environment where if they overdose, they have the chance of being revived. It’s unfair to justify not having these sites simply because people may think that these sites are just enabling addicts. 

A secret, illegal supervised injection site which the location cannot be disclosed of has overseen over 9,000 injections that otherwise would have been done in public; in this specific site, 26 overdose events were reversed by staff using the drug naloxone, with zero fatalities (Zwarenstein). This site itself can help prove that these injection sites save lives and help prevent public injections.

Illegality of sites in the United States

Before the year 2021, the United States had no legal injection sites until two opened up in New York City in late 2021 (Mays). These sites are legal under state and local jurisdiction but are not legal under federal law (Appellate Court). The staff at these sites, on the first day of being open, reversed two overdoses (Mays). Just like decriminalization of marijuana, if it is legal and allowed at the state/local level, then it doesn’t matter what the Federal law says as long as it’s within enough scope.

The fight for safe injection sites

Support for sites

Many countries such as Canada have adopted safe injection sites as a tool to help curve and treat addiction within certain cities such as Vancouver, Ontario, and Toronto. These sites are a great step to helping addicts stay clean and get the help that they need. Some sites are even offering test strips to individuals to test for Fentanyl, a powerful opioid that is 50x stronger than heroin (Kornfield).

Why safe injection sites will ultimately help us

            Safe injection sites are a crucial part of the beginning of the recovery process of addiction. Giving drug users an area to use clean supplies, in the presence of trained medical staff in case of an overdose is a way to prevent overdose and spread of disease. Not only that, but once the user’s high subsides, they are offered services that can help them get out of their addiction and on the road to recovery. Like Doctor Sarah Wakeman from Harvard Medical School said, "Many heroin users abstain from use while taking buprenorphine, but the daily decision to take it is tough" (Wakeman). With these sites, individuals who use intravenous drugs can be safer, more secure, and hopefully in the end can find the road to recovery.
